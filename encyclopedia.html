Geological condition: IKIBID 15 is a water world, its surface completely covered by vast, deep oceans. The planet’s geology is primarily shaped by its active tectonic and geothermal processes beneath the ocean floor. With no landmasses above the water, all of IKIBID 15’s geological activity occurs below the surface, from deep ocean trenches to towering underwater volcanoes. The planet’s active tectonic plates create a highly dynamic environment where the seafloor is constantly reshaped by volcanic activity and plate movements.

IKIBID 15’s oceans reach extraordinary depths, far surpassing those of Earth's, with some trenches extending tens of kilometers below the surface. These oceanic trenches are formed by tectonic subduction zones, where plates collide and one sinks beneath the other into the planet’s mantle. The immense pressures at these depths create extreme environments, inhospitable to most life forms, but rich in geological phenomena such as the formation of deep-sea mineral deposits and hydrothermal vent systems.

Volcanic ridges stretch across the seafloor, formed by magma rising from beneath the planet’s crust at mid-ocean ridges. These ridges mark the boundaries between diverging tectonic plates, where new crust is continuously formed as lava emerges from the mantle. Shield volcanoes rise from the ocean floor, though none break the surface due to the immense depth of the oceans. Their lava flows contribute to the expansion of basaltic plains across the seafloor.

Hydrothermal vents are a dominant feature of IKIBID 15's underwater landscape. These vents, concentrated around tectonic and volcanic hotspots, release superheated water rich in chemicals such as hydrogen sulfide and methane. These chemicals provide the energy needed to support chemosynthetic ecosystems. The vents themselves form towering mineral chimneys, or "black smokers," which release dense plumes of hot, mineral-laden water into the frigid ocean depths.

Without land or surface exposure, the lack of atmospheric weathering or erosion means that the oceanic crust remains relatively pristine. Geologic features on the seafloor, such as volcanic ridges and mineral deposits, persist for long periods, slowly shaped by volcanic activity and plate tectonics. Massive underwater cave systems also exist, particularly around ancient volcanic regions, where lava tubes have formed extensive subterranean networks beneath the ocean floor.


DARK OXYGEN PRODUCTION 
Dark oxygen production refers to the generation of oxygen in environments where sunlight is absent, primarily occurring in deep ocean ecosystems, subsurface habitats, or other environments devoid of light. Unlike photosynthesis, which relies on sunlight to split water molecules and release oxygen, dark oxygen production is driven by chemical energy through processes such as chemosynthesis. This phenomenon is particularly important in ecosystems near hydrothermal vents and within anoxic or low-light environments.    

Chemosynthetic bacteria and archaea, and even abiotic elements such as Manganese are central to dark oxygen production, utilize the oxidation of inorganic compounds—such as hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), methane (CH₄), or ammonia (NH₃)—to produce energy. In certain cases, these processes result in oxygen generation. For example, sulfur-oxidizing bacteria can use reduced sulfur compounds in the presence of water, releasing oxygen as a byproduct. Clumps of Manganese metal can also produce oxygen from a process called radiolysis. Radiolysis works almost exactly the same compared to electrolysis, except the disintegration of the H2O molecule is caused by the release of ionized energy from the Manganese Metals, these releases of ionized energy from radioactive materials caused Oxygen atoms to detach from the water (H2O) compound, releasing oxygen to the water.

Dark oxygen production is most commonly associated with deep-sea hydrothermal vent systems. These vents release superheated, mineral-rich water from beneath the ocean floor, providing a chemical energy source for chemosynthetic bacteria. These bacteria form the basis of the food chain in these lightless environments, supporting diverse communities of organisms, such as the Globulus. The oxygen produced through chemosynthetic reactions contributes to the local oxygen cycle, enabling organisms to thrive in these otherwise inhospitable conditions.

Planetary system: The planet is located relatively close to the other star systems. The TEL-10T System consists of a single G-type main-sequence star, emitting a steady stream of radiation and warmth typical of a yellow Star. IKIBID 15, the only planet in this system, orbits its star at a significant distance of approximately 3,2 AU, placing it at the outer edge of the star’s habitable zone. Despite the star’s moderate luminosity, IKIBID 15’s atmosphere is only half as dense as Earth’s, resulting in minimal heat retention and almost no sunlight reaching the planet’s surface. This low atmospheric density, combined with a thick cloud layer, creates a perpetually dim and cold environment where surface temperatures remain largely regulated by geothermal and chemical processes. Due to the absence of other planets or moons in the system, IKIBID 15 follows a stable, undisturbed orbit, allowing life to evolve in unique isolation. Organisms on the planet have adapted to the scarcity of sunlight by relying on chemosynthesis, extracting energy from geothermal sources rather than solar input. The G-type star, while more luminous than a red dwarf, provides little direct influence on the planet’s surface conditions, as the distance and atmospheric composition block much of its radiation. HOW LIFE STARTED IN IKIBID-15?
It is hypothesized that The origin of life on  IKIBID-15 would likely have been driven by a combination of its unique geochemical and tectonic conditions, taking place in the planet’s deep oceans, where hydrothermal vent systems provided an environment rich in chemical energy. Given the absence of sunlight,  IKIBID-15’s life would not follow the pathways of Earth’s photoautotrophic origins but would instead rely on chemosynthesis as the primary energy source

1. Geochemical Conditions for Abiogenesis:

On Xalora, the process of abiogenesis—the transition from simple chemistry to life—would have been centered around the dynamic interplay between its thin crust, active tectonic systems, and abundant geothermal energy. The hydrothermal vent systems, formed by the planet’s tectonic and volcanic activity, would release superheated water rich in chemicals like hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), methane (CH₄), ammonia (NH₃), and various metal ions. These chemical compounds, in conjunction with high-pressure, high-temperature conditions, would serve as a substrate for prebiotic chemistry.

At the molecular level, these vent environments would enable the formation of basic organic molecules like amino acids, nucleotides, and simple lipids. The continuous cycling of chemicals through thermal gradients and the high flux of dissolved minerals would facilitate complex chemical reactions, leading to the spontaneous polymerization of these molecules. Clay minerals and metal sulfides present near these vents could act as catalysts, promoting the formation of more complex organic structures such as peptides and ribonucleic acid (RNA)-like molecules.


2. Catalysis and the Role of Metal Sulfides:

The abiogenesis process on Xalora may have been highly dependent on catalytic surfaces provided by minerals such as iron-sulfur (Fe-S) and nickel-sulfur (Ni-S) compounds, abundant in hydrothermal vent environments. These metal-sulfide surfaces are known to facilitate the reduction and binding of carbon molecules in a process similar to the primordial metabolic pathways hypothesized in the Iron-Sulfur World Hypothesis. In Xalora’s high-pressure, chemically rich oceanic environment, these metal catalysts could have driven the reduction of simple carbon compounds like carbon dioxide (CO₂) into more complex organic molecules.

This catalytic process would have been an essential precursor to the development of metabolic networks. Early protocells on Xalora would have used these mineral interfaces to carry out energy-harvesting reactions, effectively setting the stage for the emergence of life that can self-replicate and metabolize without the need for sunlight.
